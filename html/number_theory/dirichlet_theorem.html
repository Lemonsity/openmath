Dirichlet Character
Consider the multiplicative group of \( q \) for \( q \in\mathbb{Z}\). A homomorphism between the 2 groups \(\chi:(\mathbb{Z}/ q\mathbb{Z})^\times\rightarrow\mathbb{C}^\times\) is called a Dirichlet Character modulus q if \(\forall a,b\in\mathbb{Z}\):
  1. \(\chi(ab)=\chi(a)\chi(b)\), i.e.\(\chi\) is completely multiplicative (also directly by property of homomorphism)
  2. \(\chi(a)=\begin{cases} 0 & gcd(a,q)>1 \\ \chi(a \text{ mod q}) & gcd(a,q)=1 \end{cases}\)
  3. \(\chi(a+q)=\chi(a)\), i.e. \(\chi\) is periodic with period q

    Remark:
  1. For every \(q\in\mathbb{Z}\), we defined a trivial character as \(\chi_0(g)=1,\,\forall g\in (\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^\times\). This is also called principle character mod q.
  2. Since by the fact that \(|(\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^\times|=\phi(q)\) and Euler’s theorem, \(\forall a\in(\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^\times\), \(\chi(a^{\phi(q)})=\chi(a)^{\phi(q)}=1\). Thus \(\forall a\in(\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z})^\times\), \(\chi(a)\) is a \(\phi(q)\) th roots of unity.

Dirichlet Theorem
For any natural number q, and a reduced residue class a (mod q), there are infinitely many primes p ≡ a (mod q). Namely, \(\lim_{s\to 1^+} \sum_{p\equiv a(mod\,q)}\frac{1}{p^s}=\infty\)
Dirichlet L-function
For \(s\in\mathbb{C}\) with \(\Re(s)>1\), the Dirichlet L-function attached to character \(\chi\) is defined as \(L(s,\chi)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\chi(n)}{n^s}\).

Remark:

  1. The Riemann zeta‐function is a special case of L-function where the character attached is the trivial character, namely \(L(s,\chi_0)=\zeta(s)=\sum^{\infty}_{n=1}\frac{1}{n^s}\)
  2. Dirichlet L-function is absolutely convergent for \(\Re(s)>1\).
  3. Just as the Riemann zeta‐function, the Dirichlet L-function can also be analytically continued to a meromorphic function with simple pole at \(s=1\) and statisfied the functional equation.
