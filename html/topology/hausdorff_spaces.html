T1 Space
A space \( X \) is called \( T _ 1 \) iff for every \( x \neq y \in X \) there exists \( U \in \mathcal{ T } _ X \) such that \( x \notin U \) and \( y \in U \)

There are a few ways of saying the above, such as the set \( X \setminus \left\{ x \right\} \) is open, another being that \( X \) can separate two different points with an open set.

Every one point set is Closed in a Hausdorff Space
Suppose that \( p \in X \) where \( X \) is a Hausdorff space, then \( \left\{ p \right\} \) is closed

We will show that \( \left\{ p \right\} \) is closed by showing \( \left\{ p \right\} = \overline{ \left\{ p \right\} } \)

Suppose that \( y \in X \) and \( y \neq p \), since \( X \) was Hausdorff, we get \( U_p, U_y \) disjoint neighborhoods. Since \( U_y \cap U_p = \emptyset \) , and \( U_p \supseteq \left\{ p \right\} \), then \( U_y \cap \left\{ p \right\} = \emptyset \) therefore \( y \notin \overline{ \left\{ p \right\} } \). Note that we've just shown that for any point in \( X \) which is not \( p \) it cannot be in the closure.

Now we show that \( p \) is actually in there. Since \( \left\{ p \right\} \subseteq \overline{ \left\{ p \right\} } \), then we know \( p \in \overline{ \left\{ p \right\} } \), so therefore \( \overline{ \left\{ p \right\} } = \left\{ p \right\} \) as needed.

A Topological Space Is Hausdorff Iff the Diagonal Is Closed
Suppose that \( X \) is a topological space, then \( X \) is hausdorff iff \( \Delta = \left\{ \left( x, x \right): x \in X \right\} \) is closed in \( X \times X \)

Suppose that \( \Delta \) is closed in \( X \times X \), now let \( x \neq y \in X \), if we consider the point \( p = \left( x, y \right) \in X \times X \) then we know that \( p \notin \Delta \), therefore \( p \in X \times X \setminus \Delta \), since \( \Delta \) was closed, then we know that \( X \times X \setminus \Delta \) is open, and therefore since \( p \) is in there, then there exists a basis element \( U \times V \) where \( U, V \) are open in \( X \) which contains \( p \) and is a subset of \( X \times X \setminus \Delta \). To show that the space is hausdorff it remains to show that \( U \cap V = \emptyset \), so suppose for the sake of contradiction that there was an element \( d \in U \cap V \) which implies that since \( \left( d, d \right) \in U \times V \) but \( U \times V \subseteq X \times X \setminus \Delta \) which shows that such an element \( d \) is impossible to exist, and therefore we must have \( U \cap V = \emptyset \)

Now suppose that \( X \) is hausdorff, let's prove that \( \Delta \) is closed in \( X \times X \), so we will prove that \( X \times X \setminus \Delta \) is open, so let \( p \in X \times X \setminus \Delta \), we have \( p = \left( x, y \right) \) where \( x \neq y \in X \) therefore since \( X \) is hausdorff then we get open neighborhoods \( U, V \) of \( x, y \) respectively that are disjoint, so that \( U \cap V = \emptyset \) this shows that \( \left( U \times V \right) \cap \Delta = \emptyset \) as if it was not empty, then \( U, V \) would no longer be disjoint, therefore we know that \( p \in U \times V \subseteq X \times X \setminus \Delta \), so that \( X \times X \setminus \Delta \) is open and thus \( \Delta \) is closed, as needed.

Pairwise Disjoint Sets for a Collection of Points in a Hausdorff Space
Suppose that \( X \) is a hausdorff space, and that \( x_1, \ldots, x _n \) are distinct elements of \( X \) then there exists open sets \( U_1, \ldots, U_n \) in \( X \) that are pairwise disjoint where \( x _i \in U_i \)

For the base case of \( n = 2 \) it follows directly from the definition of a hausdorff space, now let \( k \in \mathbb{ N } _2 \) and suppose that the statement holds true on \( k \) elements, now we'll show that it holds true on \( k + 1 \) elements. Let \( C = { x_1, \ldots, x_{k + 1} } \) be the \( k + 1 \) elements, let \( C_i = C \setminus \left\{ x_i \right\} \), which produces a set of size \( k \) on which we know the induction hypothesis holds true, and therefore we obtain \( U _ { 1, i }, \ldots , U _ {k, i} \) disjoint open sets such that \( x_j \in U_{j, i} \) for all \( j \neq i \) and lets call the family of these sets by the mask of \( C_i \)

By iterating \( i \) over \( 1, \ldots k + 1 \) then we can form smaller open sets around each \( x _ j \) by taking \( \bigcap _ i U _ { j, i} \), they are open as they are finite intersections of open sets, and we can show that they pairwise disjoint, for consider any \( a \neq b \in [1, \ldots , n] \) and consider \( \bigcap _i U_{a, i} \) and \( \bigcap _i U_{b, i} \), since \( k + 1 \ge 3 \) then we can consider some \( m \in [1, \ldots , k + 1] \) such that \( m \neq a \) and \( m \neq b \) then consider the \( m \)-th mask, \( U_{1, m}, \ldots U_{k, m} \) of \( C_m \) as noted in the previous paragraph the induction hypothesis shows these are pairwise disjoint, additionally we know that \( \bigcap _i U_{a, i} \subseteq U_{a, m} \) and that \( \bigcup _i U_{b, i} \subseteq U_{b, m} \) (this is because they are part of the intersection), now since \( U_{a, m} \) and \( U_{b, m} \) are disjoint then are the intersections in question, thus we've shown that the sets \( \left\{ x \in [1, \ldots , k + 1]: \bigcup _i U_{x, i} \right\} \) are pairwise disjoint and cover \( x _1, \ldots , x_{k + 1} \), as needed, therefore by the principle of induction this holds true for all \( n \in \mathbb{ N } _ 2 \)

Every Ordered Set With the Order Topology Is Hausdorff
Suppose that \( Y_< \) is the order topology, then \( Y \) is hausdorff, moreover given \( a, b \in Y \) and their disjoint neihborhoods, \( U, V \) then we have that for any \( u, v \in U, V \) respectively that \( u \lt v \)

Let \( a, b \in Y \) and assume that \( a \lt b \).

Case 1: \( Y \) contains no smallest or largest element, then there exists some \( j, k \in Y \) such that \( j \lt a \) and \( b \lt k \) now if there is no \( y \in Y \) such that \( a \lt y \lt b \) then consider \( U = \left( j, b \right), V = \left( a, k \right) \), we can see that \( a \in U, b \in V \) and that \( U \cap V = \emptyset \), also consider any \( u, v \in U, V \) this implies that \( u = a \) or \( u \lt a \) and that \( v = b \) or \( v \gt b \) in any case we know that \( u \lt v \). If there was some \( y \) between \( a, b \) then we let \( U = \left( j, y \right), V = \left( y, k \right) \).

Case 2: \( Y \) contains a smallest element but no largest element, if it turns out that \( a \) is not the smallest element, then suppose that \( s \lt a \) is, since there is no largest element then we know that there is an element \( k \) such that \( b \lt k \), if there is no \( y \) between \( a, b \) then the sets \( U = [s, b) \) \( V = \left( a, k \right) \), if there is a \( y \) we take \( U = [s, y], V = (y, b) \). If it turned out that \( a \) was the smallest element, we adjust the above with syntactically replacing \( s \) with \( a \).

Case 3: \( Y \) contains a largest and smallest element if neither of \( a, b \) are the largest or smallest element then we obtain \( j, k \) which are and follow the case 1 construction, if exactly one of \( a, b \) is the largest or smallest element, then we proceed with the case 2 construction, finally when \( a, b \) are the smallest and largest element respectively, then again if there is some \( y \) between \( a, b \) then we can use \( U = [a, y), V = (y, b] \) otherwise we use \( U = [a, b), V = (a, b] \) .

Thus we've proven that in any case \( a, b \) had open sets containing them that were disjoint.
