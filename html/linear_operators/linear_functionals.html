Linear Functional
Suppose that \( V \) is a vector space over a field \( K \in \left\{ \mathbb{ R } , \mathbb{ C } \right\} \), then a linear functional is a linear operator \( f : V \to K \)

Note that in the case of the reals or complex we have the norm as the absolute value. Also we say that a linear functional is real if \( F = \mathbb{ R } \) and say that it is complex if \( F = \mathbb{ C } \)

Bounded Linear Functional
We say that a linear functional \( f \) is bounded when it is a bounded linear operator

The above equates to saying that there exists some \( c \in \mathbb{ R } ^ + \) such that for all \( x \in \operatorname{ dom } \left( f \right) , \left\lvert f \left( x \right) \right\rvert \le c \left\lVert x \right\rVert \)

Dual Space

A Schauder basis for \(l^p\) is \(\left(e_k\right)\), where \(e_k=\left(\delta_{k i}\right)\) as in the preceding example. Then every \(x \in l^p\) has a unique representation (9) \[ x=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \xi_k e_k \] Given any \( f \) in the dual space of \(l^p\), since it is \(f\) is linear and bounded we have: \[ f(x)=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \xi_k \gamma_k \quad \gamma_k=f\left(e_k\right) \] Let \(q\) be the conjugate of \(p\) (cf. 1.2-3) and consider \(x_n=\left(\xi_k^{(n)}\right)\) with \[ \xi_k^{(n)}=\left\{\begin{array}{cl} \left|\gamma_k\right|^q / \gamma_k & \text { if } k \leqq n \text { and } \gamma_k \neq 0 \\ 0 & \text { if } k>n \text { or } \quad \gamma_k=0 \end{array}\right. \] Thus we have: \[ f\left(x_n\right)=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \xi_k^{(n)} \gamma_k=\sum_{k=1}^n\left|\gamma_k\right|^4 \] We also have, using (11) and \((q-1) p=q\), \[ \begin{aligned} f\left(x_n\right) \leqq\|f\|\left\|x_n\right\| & =\|f\|\left(\sum\left|\xi_k^{(n)}\right|^p\right)^{1 / p} \\ & =\|f\|\left(\sum\left|\gamma_k\right|^{(q-1) p}\right)^{1 / p} \\ & =\|f\|\left(\sum\left|\gamma_k\right|^q\right)^{1 / p} \end{aligned} \] (sum from 1 to \(n\) ). We will now show that the norm of \(f\) is the norm on the space \(l^{\infty}\) \[ \left\lvert f \left( x \right) \right\rvert = \left\lvert \sum _ { k = 1 } ^ \infty c _ k f \left( e _ k \right) \right\rvert \le \sup _ j \left\lvert f \left( e _ k \right) \right\rvert \sum _ { k = 1 } ^ \infty \left\lvert c _ k \right\rvert = \left\lVert x \right\rVert \sup _ j \left\lvert f \left( e _ j \right) \right\rvert \] Therefore when we take this supremum over all \( x \) on the unit circle we attain that: \[ \left\lVert f \right\rVert _ \operatorname{ op } \le \sup _ j \left\lvert f \left( e _ k \right) \right\rvert \] But earlier in the proof we had the other direction of the above inequality so we have \( \left\lVert f \right\rVert _ \operatorname{ op } = \sup _ j \left\lvert f \left( e _ j \right) \right\rvert \) which is the \( l ^ \infty \) norm, that is we have \( \left\lVert f \right\rVert _ \operatorname{ op } = \left\lVert y \right\rVert _ \infty \) where \( y = \left( f \left( e _ k \right) \right) \in l ^ \infty \), showing that the mapping \( f \mapsto \left( y \right) \) is a bijective linear mapping of \( l ^ 1 \) onto \( l ^ \infty \) therefore it is an isomorphism, as needed.
Hahn Banach
Let \(X\) be a real vector space, \(p\) a sublinear functional on \(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{M}\) a subspace of \(\mathcal{X}\), and \(f\) a linear functional on \(\mathcal{M}\) such that \(f(x) \leq p(x)\) for all \(x \in \mathcal{M}\). Then there exists a linear functional \(F\) on \(\mathcal{X}\) such that \(F(x) \leq p(x)\) for all \(x \in \mathcal{X}\) and \(F \mid \mathcal{M}=f\).
We begin by showing that if \(x \in X \backslash 2 \mathrm{M}, f\) can be extended to a linear functional \(g\) on \(\mathcal{M}+\mathbb{R} x\) satisfying \(g(y) \leq p(y)\) there. If \(y_1, y_2 \in \mathcal{M}\), we have \[ f\left(y_1\right)+f\left(y_2\right)=f\left(y_1+y_2\right) \leq p\left(y_1+y_2\right) \leq p\left(y_1-x\right)+p\left(x+y_2\right), \] or \[ f\left(y_1\right)-p\left(y_1-x\right) \leq p\left(x+y_2\right)-f\left(y_2\right) . \] Hence \[ \sup \{f(y)-p(y-x): y \in \mathcal{M}\} \leq \inf \{p(x+y)-f(y): y \in \mathcal{M}\} . \] Let \(\alpha\) be any number satisfying \[ \sup \{f(y)-p(y-x): y \in \mathcal{N}\} \leq \alpha \leq \inf \{p(x+y)-f(y): y \in \mathcal{M}\} \] and define \(g: \mathcal{M}+\mathbb{R} x \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) by \(g(y+\lambda x)=f(y)+\lambda \alpha\). Then \(g\) is clearly linear, and \(g \mid \mathcal{M}=f\), so that \(g(y) \leq p(y)\) for \(y \in \mathcal{M}\). Moreover, if \(\lambda>0\) and \(y \in \mathcal{M}\). \[ g(y+\lambda x)=\lambda[f(y / \lambda)+\alpha] \leq \lambda[f(y / \lambda)+p(x+(y / \lambda))-f(y / \lambda)]=p(y+\lambda x), \] whereas if \(\lambda=-\mu<0\), \[ g(y+\lambda w)-\mu[f(y / \mu)-a] \leq \mu[f(y / \mu)-f(y / \mu)+p((y / \mu)-x)]=p(y+\lambda x) \] Thus \(g(z) \leq p(z)\) for all \(z \in \mathcal{M}+\mathbb{R} x\). Evidently the same reasoning can be applied to any linear extension \(F\) of \(f\) satisfying \(F \leq p\) on its domain, and it shows that the domain of a maximal linear extension satisfying \(F \leq p\) must be the whole space \(X\). But the family \(\mathcal{F}\) of all tinear extensions \(F\) of \(f\) satisfying \(F \leq p\) is partially ordered by inclusion (maps from subspaces of \(X\) to \(\mathbb{R}\) being regarded as subsets of \(X \times \mathbb{R}\) ). Since the union of any increasing family of subspaces of \(X\) is agath a subspace, one casily sees that the union of a linearly ordered subfamily of \(\mathcal{F}\) lies in \(\mathcal{F}\). The proof is therefore completed by invoking Zom's lemma.

5.7 The Complex Hahn-Banach Theorem. Let \(\mathcal{X}\) be a complex vector space, \(p\) a seminorm on \(X, \mathcal{M}\) a subspace of \(\mathcal{X}\), and \(f\) a complex linear functional on \(\mathcal{M}\) such that \(|f(x)| \leq p(x)\) for \(x \in \mathcal{M}\). Then there exists a complex linear functional \(F\) on \(X\) such that \(|F(x)| \leq p(x)\) for all \(x \in \mathcal{X}\) and \(F \mid \mathcal{M}=f\). Proof. Let \(u=\operatorname{Re} f\). By Theorem 5.6 there is a real linear extension \(U\) of \(u\) to \(X\) such that \(|U(x)| \leq p(x)\) for all \(x \in X\). Let \(F(x)=U(x)-i U(i x)\) as in Proposition 5.5. Then \(F\) is a complex linear extension of \(f\), and as in the proof of Proposition 5.5, if \(\alpha=\overline{\operatorname{sgn} F(x)}\) we have \(|F(x)|=\alpha F(x)=F(\alpha x)=U(\alpha x) \leq p(\alpha x)=p(x)\).

On the other hand, (b) implies that \(\|\widehat{2}\| \geq\|x\|\). With notation as in Theorem 5.8d, let \(\widehat{X}=\{\hat{x}: x \in X\}\). Since \(x^*\) is always complete, the closure \(\overline{\widehat{X}}\) of \(\widehat{X}\) in \(X^{* *}\) is a Banach space, and the map \(x \mapsto \widehat{x}\) embeds \(x\) into \(\bar{X}\) as a dense subspace. \(\bar{x}\) is called the completion of \(X\). In particular, if \(X\) is itself a Banach space then \(\bar{x}=\widehat{x}\). If \(x\) is finite-dimensional, then of course \(\widehat{x}=x^{* *}\), since these spaces have the same dimension. For infinite-dimensional Banach spaces it may or may not happen that \(\hat{x}=x \cdot *\); if it does, \(x\) is called reflexive. The examples of Banach spaces we have examined so far are not reflexive except in trivial cases where they turn out to be finite-dimensional. We shall prove some cases of this assertion and present examples of reflexive Banach spaces in later sections. Usually we shall identify \(\widehat{x}\) with \(x\) and thus regard \(x^{* *}\) as a superspace of \(x\); reflexivity then means that \(x^{* *}=x\)
