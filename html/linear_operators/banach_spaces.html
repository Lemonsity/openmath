Norm on a Vector Space
Suppose that \( V \) is a vector space a subfield \(F\) of the complex numbers \(\mathbb{C}\) a norm is a function \( \left\lVert \cdot \right\rVert : V \to \mathbb{ R } \) such that
  1. \(\left\lVert x + y \right\rVert \leq \left\lVert x \right\rVert + \left\lVert y \right\rVert \) for all \(x, y \in X\).
  2. for any \( c \in F \), we have \(\left\lVert cx \right\rVert = \left\lvert c \right\rvert \left\lVert x \right\rVert \) for all \(x \in V\) and
  3. for all \(x \in X\), if \( \left\lVert x \right\rVert =0\) then \(x=0\).
Norm Is Zero Iff Input Is Zero
Suppose that \( \left\lVert \cdot \right\rVert : V \to \mathbb{ R } \) is a norm, then we have for any \( v \in V \) \[ \left\lVert v \right\rVert = 0 _ F \iff v = 0 _ V \]

We already know that if \( \left\lVert v \right\rVert = 0 \) then \( v = 0 \) by property 3 of the norm. Now we verify that the norm of zero is zero, but it is because we have: \( \left\lVert 0 _ V \right\rVert = \left\lVert 0 _ F \cdot 0 _ V \right\rVert = 0 _ F \left\lVert 0 _V \right\rVert = 0 _ F \).

Non-negativity of the Norm
Suppose that \( \left\lVert \cdot \right\rVert : V \to \mathbb{ R } \) is a norm, then the following is true for every \( v \in V \) \[ 0 \le \left\lVert v \right\rVert \]
Using properties 1 and 2 we have \[ 0 = \left\lVert v - v \right\rVert \le \left\lVert v \right\rVert + \left\lVert -v \right\rVert = 2 \left\lVert v \right\rVert \] thus by division by 2 we deduce that \( 0 \le \left\lVert v \right\rVert \) as needed.

Linear Independence Coefficient Inequality
Let \(\left\{x_1, \cdots, x_n\right\}\) be a linearly independent set of vectors in a normed space \(X\) (of any dimension). Then there is a number \(c>0\) such that for every choice of scalars \(\alpha_1, \cdots, \alpha_n\) we have \[ \left\|\alpha_1 x_1+\cdots+\alpha_n x_n\right\| \geq c\left(\left|\alpha_1\right|+\cdots+\left|\alpha_n\right|\right) \]
We write \(s=\left|\alpha_1\right|+\cdots+\left|\alpha_n\right|\). If \(s=0\), all \(\alpha_i\) are zero, so that (1) holds for any \(c\). Let \(s>0\). Then (1) is equivalent to the inequality which we obtain from (1) by dividing by \(s\) and writing \(\beta_i=\alpha_i / s\), that is, \[ \left\|\beta_1 x_1+\cdots+\beta_n x_n\right\| \geqq c \quad \quad\left(\sum_{j=1}^n\left|\beta_j\right|=1\right) . \] Hence it suffices to prove the existence of a \(c>0\) such that (2) holds for every \(n\)-tuple of scalars \(\beta_1, \cdots, \beta_n\) with \(\sum\left|\beta_j\right|=1\). Suppose that this is false. Then there exists a sequence \(\left(y_m\right)\) of vectors \[ y_m=\beta_1^{(m)} x_1+\cdots+\beta_n^{(m)} x_n \quad\left(\sum_{j=1}^n\left|\beta_i^{(m)}\right|=1\right) \] such that \[ \left\|y_m\right\| \longrightarrow 0 \quad \text { as } m \longrightarrow \infty \] Now we reason as follows. Since \(\sum\left|\boldsymbol{\beta}_j^{(m)}\right|=1\), we have \(\left|\boldsymbol{\beta}_j^{(m)}\right| \leqq 1\). Hence for each fixed \(j\) the sequence \[ \left(\beta_j^{(m)}\right)=\left(\beta_j^{(1)}, \beta_j^{(2)}, \cdots\right) \] is bounded. Consequently, by the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem, \(\left(\boldsymbol{\beta}_1^{(m)}\right.\) ) has a convergent subsequence. Let \(\beta_1\) denote the limit of that subsequence, and let ( \(y_{1, m}\) ) denote the corresponding subsequence of \(\left(y_m\right)\). By the same argument, \(\left(y_{1, m}\right)\) has a subsequence \(\left(y_{2, m}\right)\) for which the corresponding subsequence of scalars \(\beta_2^{(m)}\) converges; let \(\beta_2\) denote the limit. Continuing in this way, after \(n\) steps we obtain a subsequence \(\left(y_{n, m}\right)=\left(y_{n, 1}, y_{n, 2}, \cdots\right)\) of \(\left(y_m\right)\) whose terms are of the form \[ y_{n, m}=\sum_{j=1}^n \gamma_j^{(m)} x_j \quad\left(\sum_{j=1}^n\left|\gamma_j^{(m)}\right|=1\right) \] with scalars \(\gamma_j^{(m)}\) satisfying \(\gamma_i^{(m)} \longrightarrow \beta_i\) as \(m \longrightarrow \infty\). Hence, as \(m \longrightarrow \infty\), \[ y_{n, m} \longrightarrow y=\sum_{j=1}^n \beta_i x_i \] where \(\sum\left|\beta_j\right|=1\), so that not all \(\beta_j\) can be zero. Since \(\left\{x_1, \cdots, x_n\right\}\) is a linearly independent set, we thus have \(y \neq 0\). On the other hand, \(y_{n, m} \longrightarrow y\) implies \(\left\|y_{n, m}\right\| \longrightarrow\|y\|\), by the continuity of the norm. Since \(\left\|y_m\right\| \longrightarrow 0\) by assumption and \(\left(y_{n, m}\right)\) is a subsequence of \(\left(y_m\right)\), we must have \(\left\|y_{n, m}\right\| \longrightarrow 0\). Hence \(\|y\|=0\), so that \(y=0\) by (N2) in Sec. 2.2. This contradicts \(y \neq 0\), and the lemma is proved.

If the Closed Unit Ball Is Compact Then the Space Is Finite Dimensional
If a normed space \(X\) has the property that the closed unit ball \( M = \{x : \left\lVert x \right\rVert \leq 1 \} \) is compact, then \(X\) is finite dimensional
For the sake of contradiction assume that \(M\) is compact but \(\operatorname{dim} X=\infty\). We choose any \( x_1 \in X \) of norm 1. This \(x_1\) generates a one dimensional subspace \(X_1\) of \(X\), and is therefore closed and is a proper subspace of \(X\) since \(\operatorname{dim} X=\infty\). By Riesz's lemma there is an \(x_2 \in X\) of norm 1 such that \[ \left\|x_2-x_1\right\| \geqq \theta=\frac{1}{2} . \] The elements \(x_1, x_2\) generate a two dimensional proper closed subspace \(X_2\) of \(X\). By Riesz's lemma there is an \(x_3\) of norm 1 such that for all \(x \in X_2\) we have \[ \left\|x_3-x\right\| \geqq \frac{1}{2} . \] In particular, \[ \begin{align*} & \left\|x_3-x_1\right\| \geq \frac{1}{2}, \\ & \left\|x_3-x_2\right\| \geq \frac{1}{2} . \end{align*} \] Proceeding by induction, we obtain a sequence ( \(x_n\) ) of elements \(x_n \in M\) such that \[ \left\|x_m-x_n\right\| \geqq \frac{1}{2} \quad(m \neq n) . \] Obviously, \(\left(x_n\right)\) cannot have a convergent subsequence. This contradicts the compactness of \(M\). Hence our assumption \(\operatorname{dim} X=\infty\) is false, and \(\operatorname{dim} X<\infty\).

Bounded Linear Operator
Suppose that \( L : X \to Y \) is a linear operator, then we say that it is bounded diff \( \exists M \in \mathbb{ R } ^ + \) such that for any \( x \in X \), we have \[ \left\lVert Lx \right\rVert _Y \le M \left\lVert x \right\rVert _X \]

The intuitive meaning is that there is some scalar such that the linear operator can only increase the length of the vector by that scalar multiple. For example a linear transform that multiplies all vector by some constant, will trivially satisfy this. We can re-obtain our familiar notion of bounededness by seeing that any bounded subset of \( X \) becomes a bouneded subset of \( Y \).

But note that even though the above paragraph is true, it's still not the same as the regular notion of boundedness we encounter in calculus, where we had that the range of the function is a bounded set. This is different because our definition of bounded above may not have the range being bounded.

On a new note, if we ask ourselves what value of \( M \) is the smallest such that the equation \( \left\lVert Lx \right\rVert _Y \le M \left\lVert x \right\rVert _X \) holds true for all \( x \in X \). For a given value \( x _ 0 \) if the smallest value of \( M \) where the equation holds true is \( M _ 0 \) then the smallest value that works for all \( x \in X \) is at least \( M _ 0 \).

Therefore by first noting that when \( x = 0 \) then \( T x = 0 \) and therefore any value of \( M \) works for \( x = 0 \), specifically \( M = 0 \) works, and thus yields no constraint on the value of \( M \) that works for all \( x \in X \) then we may restrict our search on values of \( x \in X \) such that \( X \neq 0 \) therefore we are looking for an \( M \) such that \( \frac{ \left\lVert T x \right\rVert }{ \left\lVert x \right\rVert } \le M \) for all \( x \in X \).

A Linear Operator With a Finite Dimensional Domain Is Bounded
If a normed space \(X\) is finite dimensional, then every linear operator on \(X\) is bounded.
Proof. Let \(\operatorname{dim} X=n\) and \(\left\{e_1, \cdots, e_n\right\}\) a basis for \(X\). We take any \(x=\sum \xi_j e_j\) and consider any linear operator \(T\) on \(X\). Since \(T\) is linear, \[ \|T x\|=\left\|\sum \xi_i T e_i\right\| \leqq \sum\left|\xi_i\right|\left\|T e_i\right\| \leqq \max _k\left\|T e_k\right\| \sum\left|\xi_j\right| \] (summations from 1 to \(n\) ). To the last sum we apply Lemma 2.4-1 with \(\alpha_i=\xi_i\) and \(x_i=e_j\). Then we obtain \[ \sum\left|\xi_i\right| \leqq \frac{1}{c}\left\|\sum \xi_i e_i\right\|=\frac{1}{c}\|x\| . \] Together, \[ \|T x\| \leqq \gamma\|x\| \quad \text { where } \quad \gamma=\frac{1}{c} \max _k\left\|T e_k\right\| . \] From this and (1) we see that \(T\) is bounded.
